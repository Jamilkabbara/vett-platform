You have an idea. Not a fully-baked product, not a market-validated PRD - an *idea*. The kind of thing that lives in a pitch deck slide or a Saturday-morning Notion doc.

The traditional advice is: go talk to customers. The problem is that you don't have customers yet. You have an idea. The audience you'd want to validate against doesn't know your name, isn't on your email list, and isn't going to answer a cold LinkedIn DM about a hypothetical product. The agency that *would* recruit them charges $5K-$50K and takes 4-12 weeks, by which point your investor lunch has already happened and the idea has either calcified or been abandoned.

This is the gap synthetic research fills. Not as a replacement for real-panel work later, but as a way to get directional signal in the first 24 hours - cheap enough to throw away, fast enough to inform the next conversation.

Here's the loop, hour by hour.

## Hour 0-1: Frame the question

The most common reason early validation fails is that founders ask the wrong question. "Is this a good idea?" produces useless data because the answer depends entirely on who you ask and how you frame it.

A useful question has three properties:

1. **Specific.** "Would you use a budgeting app?" is too broad. "Would you switch from your current budgeting app to one that auto-categorizes expenses using your bank's transaction data?" is specific.
2. **Decision-relevant.** What will you do differently if the answer is yes vs no? If nothing - skip the question.
3. **Behaviour, not aspiration.** "Do you care about X?" gets aspirational answers. "When did you last spend money trying to solve X?" gets behavioural ones.

Write down three to five questions in this format. The 24-hour loop will iterate them.

## Hour 1-3: Set up a 5-persona Sniff Test

The Sniff Test exists for exactly this stage. Five personas, $9, generated to your screener spec. The cost is the price of a sandwich. The signal is directional.

A good Sniff Test screener has:

- A market (geography + demographic floor: e.g. "UAE residents aged 25-44 with monthly household income above AED 20,000")
- A behavioural qualifier (e.g. "currently uses at least one budgeting app or spreadsheet")
- A goal-aware twist (e.g. "has switched financial apps in the last 12 months")

The screener is what makes synthetic research useful. AI personas are generated *to* your spec, not filtered against a panel of strangers. If you specify "switched apps in the last 12 months", every persona has switched apps - no waste, no drop-off attrition.

Set up the mission. Run it. The five personas respond in roughly the time it takes to make coffee.

## Hour 3-4: Read the AI synthesis, gut-check

The output isn't five raw transcripts - it's a structured synthesis: an executive summary, the dominant signal across personas, tensions where personas disagree, segment-level cross-cuts, and a recommended next step.

Two questions to ask of the synthesis:

1. **Does the dominant signal match your prior?** If yes, your hypothesis isn't dead - keep going. If no, the next hour decides whether to update the hypothesis or update the screener.
2. **Where do the personas disagree?** Tensions are where the interesting work is. "Three of five personas would switch; two said the friction of importing transaction history isn't worth it" is more useful than five "yes" responses.

The Sniff Test isn't statistically powered. It's directional. Don't over-read the percentages; read the language.

## Hour 4-8: Iterate the question, run a Confidence study

Now you've sharpened. The original question was probably too broad or too leading. Rewrite it. Tighten the screener (drop a constraint that produced flat responses, add one that creates a meaningful split).

Run a 50-persona Confidence study ($99). Larger sample, broader demographic spread, enough volume that segment-level cross-cuts become real. The mission completes in minutes.

What you're looking for at this stage:

- A consistent dominant signal across at least one segment
- Tensions that point at a *specific* product feature, not a general "people are different"
- Open-text patterns that surface a vocabulary - what words do these personas use to describe the problem? (Marketing copy lives here.)

## Hour 8-16: Sleep

This sounds frivolous; it isn't. The single highest-leverage thing you can do between two research rounds is sleep on the data and let your prior shift before the next round, not after.

Most validation failures happen because founders run round 2 with the same prior they had at round 1, then add a third round to "confirm" what they already wanted to believe. Eight hours offline breaks the pattern.

## Hour 16-24: Triage tensions, decide the next move

You're back. Re-read the Confidence synthesis with fresh eyes. By now you should be able to describe, in two sentences:

- What the data clearly shows.
- What it doesn't show, and what would close the gap.

Three branches from here:

**Branch A: ship a prototype.** The data is consistent enough that the next bottleneck is product, not validation. Build the smallest version that tests the dominant signal. This is the most common outcome of a successful 24-hour loop.

**Branch B: run a Scale study ($899).** The signal is interesting but you need 1,000 personas across multiple markets to commit to a decision the size you're about to make. Scale is for high-stakes calls - market-entry, pricing-floor, naming - not for general curiosity.

**Branch C: kill the idea.** The data killed the hypothesis. This is a *win* - it cost you 24 hours and $108, not three months and a co-founder.

## What you DON'T learn from synthetic research

Synthetic personas are useful for directional signal. They're not useful for:

- **Verified-human quotes.** If your stakeholder review needs "watch this video of a real customer struggle with the checkout flow", you need UserTesting or a real-panel agency study. Synthetic doesn't produce video, doesn't produce verified quotes, and doesn't carry the same stakeholder credibility.
- **Open-text emotional nuance on niche brands.** AI-modelled personas have weaker recall of specific sub-cultural brands than real respondents. If your study turns on whether someone has heard of a specific local fashion label or local fintech challenger, the data thins out.
- **Hard-to-reach niches.** B2B procurement leaders at Fortune 500 companies. Specific medical conditions. Security-cleared roles. These are real-panel territory.
- **Regulatory or M&A diligence.** Anything where the deliverable needs custom methodology, peer-reviewable rigour, and an agency's name on the cover - synthetic research won't pass that bar.

The honest framing: use synthetic for the iteration loops, real-panel for the launch-grade study. Most teams that do both report cutting their agency SOW cost roughly in half because the brief is concrete by the time the contract gets signed.

## Common mistakes in the 24-hour loop

- **Asking five questions.** A 5-persona Sniff Test that asks five questions produces 25 data points, none of them deep. Ask one core question and three follow-ups, not five flat ones.
- **Reading the percentages.** "60% said yes" with n=5 is meaningless. Read the language, not the numbers.
- **Skipping the sleep step.** Two rounds back-to-back at the same prior is one round of validation theatre.
- **Running Scale too early.** A 1,000-persona study on a hypothesis that hasn't survived a Sniff Test is $899 of confirmation bias.
- **Not iterating the screener.** If the personas all agreed, the screener was probably too narrow. If they all disagreed, the screener was too broad. The data tells you which.

## What "shipping" looks like

After 24 hours, you should be able to write down:

- The hypothesis you started with
- The hypothesis you ended with (probably narrower, sharper, vocabulary-aware)
- The data that updated it
- The specific next step (build / scale-validate / kill)

That's the whole point. Not "validation" as a one-shot ritual, but a fast loop that pays off in the next conversation: with a co-founder, a designer, an investor, or the version of yourself that wakes up tomorrow and has to decide what to build first.

If the loop costs $108 and 24 hours, you can run it weekly. If it costs $50K and three months, you'll run it once, defend the result, and ship the wrong thing.
