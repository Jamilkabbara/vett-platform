Research buyers keep hearing the question framed as a choice: do you trust AI personas or do you trust real panel respondents? The question is wrong. They solve different problems and the right answer is usually both, in sequence.

## The category split (not the brand split)

- Synthetic: AI-modelled personas generated to your screener, fast and cheap, optimized for iteration
- Real-panel: verified humans recruited to your screener, slow and expensive, optimized for stakeholder credibility
- The category split runs through every product in the space - not just "VETT vs SurveyMonkey" but "the synthetic category vs the panel category"

## Where real-panel research wins

- Verified-human quotes for legal / compliance / press use cases
- Stakeholder-credibility framing - "validated by [Kantar / Ipsos / Nielsen]" carries decades of trust
- Hard-to-reach niches where AI training data is thin (specific medical conditions, security-cleared roles, B2B procurement at named accounts)
- Custom methodology defensible to a peer reviewer or regulator
- Mature markets where panel-supply quality is high and CPI is reasonable

## Where synthetic research wins

- Pre-launch validation when you don't have an audience yet
- Iteration loops where you'd burn out a real list with 5-10 surveys in a week
- Strict screeners that real-panel supply struggles with (the panel is filtered against the screener; synthetic is generated to it)
- AI synthesis baked in - executive summary, tensions, cross-cut analysis - not a custom analyst step
- Markets where panel quality is patchy (MENA, parts of Africa, frontier markets)
- Price-sensitive iteration: $9-$299 covers most early-stage cycles

## The hybrid loop most teams converge on

- Round 1-3: synthetic, $9-$99 per round, ideas die or get sharper
- Round 4: synthetic at scale ($899) to confirm the dominant signal across segments
- Round 5: real-panel agency study with a *specific* brief because rounds 1-4 already burned through the questions an agency would charge $50K to discover
- Result: agency SOW typically costs less than it would have, because the brief is concrete

## Two failure modes to avoid

- **Treating synthetic as a replacement for panel.** It isn't. It's an upstream phase. If you skip the panel study before a launch, you're shipping without verified-human data, and that gap will surface when a stakeholder asks who said this.
- **Treating panel as the only valid research.** A research budget that won't fund iteration loops only funds *one* expensive study, which means the methodology has to be right on the first try. Most aren't.

## What this means for the next research project you scope

- If it's pre-launch and you're still figuring out what to ask: start with synthetic. Use the iteration loops to sharpen the brief.
- If it's launch-grade with stakeholder review attached: end with real-panel. Pay for the methodology + the analyst + the polish.
- If it's both: synthetic first, panel second, with a clear handoff between phases. Most agencies are now comfortable with this pattern; some are explicit about pricing it differently.
